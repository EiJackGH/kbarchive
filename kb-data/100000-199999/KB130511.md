# KB130511: Conflict Between Sound Blaster and Built-in MIDI

## SYMPTOMS
When you attempt to play MIDI files (.mid) or use the Media Player in Windows 95, your computer may stop responding (hang), or you may receive the following error message:

> "The MIDI device is being used by another application. Wait until it is free, and then try again."

This occurs most frequently on systems using Creative Labs Sound Blaster 16 or AWE32 sound cards in conjunction with an onboard (built-in) MIDI port.

## CAUSE
This behavior occurs because both the Sound Blaster card and the motherboard's built-in MIDI controller are attempting to use the same Input/Output (I/O) address—typically **330h** or **300h**.



## RESOLUTION
To resolve this conflict, you must disable the built-in MIDI port in the System BIOS or reconfigure the Sound Blaster's MIDI I/O address via Device Manager.

### Method 1: Change Resources in Device Manager
1. Click **Start**, point to **Settings**, and then click **Control Panel**.
2. Double-click **System**, and then click the **Device Manager** tab.
3. Double-click the **Sound, video and game controllers** branch to expand it.
4. Click your **Sound Blaster** device, and then click **Properties**.
5. Click the **Resources** tab. 
6. Uncheck the **Use automatic settings** check box.
7. Select the **Input/Output Range** that corresponds to the MIDI port (e.g., 0330-0331) and click **Change Setting**.
8. Enter a non-conflicting value (e.g., **0300**) and click **OK**.
9. Restart the computer when prompted.

### Method 2: Disable Onboard MIDI in BIOS
1. Restart your computer and enter the **CMOS Setup Utility** (usually by pressing F2, DEL, or ESC during boot).
2. Locate the **Integrated Peripherals** or **Advanced** menu.
3. Change the **Onboard MIDI Port** setting to **Disabled**.
4. Save settings and exit (usually F10).

## STATUS
Microsoft has confirmed this to be a problem in the Microsoft products listed at the beginning of this article.
